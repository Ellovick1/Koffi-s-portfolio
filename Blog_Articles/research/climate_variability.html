Climate Variability and Agricultural Productivity in USA

Abstract

Climatic aberrations including rainfall, drought and temperature have been linked with adverse influence on farm output. Intense dry weather supported with low rainfall often result to crop failures, decreasing food supplies and product price hikes. Further, as urbanization and consumption of fossils continue to surge, predictions are that, weather variability is imminent. To this end, the present study examines time-series climatic variables including precipitation, drought, average temperature and carbon emission on agricultural productivity in USA. The data set used for this study span between 1967 and 2017. Preliminary investigation shows evidence of unit root property on all variables. Having established a long run relationship among the series, the study adopted the Error Correction Modelling (ECM) technique to empirically assess the effects of climate variability on agricultural productivity. From the baseline regression result, it is evident that one period lag of average temperature and precipitation explains climate variability in the short-run. However, the error correction term is statistically significant with about 25% speed of adjustment. This implies that at a speed of 25%, the disequilibrium in farm output as a result of climate changes will be corrected annually with effective policies. Therefore, rethinking the likely harmful effects of decreasing temperature and rainfall should be a priority for the US agricultural sector.

Keywords: Climatic aberrations, Regression, Long-run relationship, Error Correction Term

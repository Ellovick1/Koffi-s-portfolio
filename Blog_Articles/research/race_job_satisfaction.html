Is There a Relationship Between Race And Job Satisfaction Among Adults Who Are Over 18years In Usa?

1.0 Introduction

In the United State of America, adults who are gainfully employed consider their job as a vital component of their livelihood other than just as a primary source of earning. Owing to the fact that work conveys some sought of social standing on the workers, physically, mentally and health wise, a considerable portion of time is devoted by all employees for work every day. Seemingly, because of the role work plays on the lives of the average worker, there is need for job satisfaction if the well-being of the employees must be achieved. Unfortunately, one cannot say with certainty that all employees in same occupation, workplace or environment achieved equal job satisfaction in the United State (Davis, James, Smith, Tom and Marsden 2007)

An avalanche of study exists on the employee job satisfaction relationship, but mostly, they seem to consider sex and other demographic variables (Sousa-Poza and Sousa-Poza 2000; Clark 1997; Sloane and Williams 2000; Pew Research Center 2013 among others). Studies on the race job satisfaction relationship are few. An attempt to examine job satisfaction and racial differences was last undertaken by Bartel (1981) when she studied the National Longitudinal Survey data to establish satisfaction nexus among the black and white work force.

According to earlier study by Bartel (1981), blacks and other race reported that they were satisfied with their job. Bartel (1981) explained that the high job satisfaction by the races other than the whites was consequent on low expectation they possess for their job other than their colour. Blacks are willing and are contented with the little given to them. However, if their low expectation is controlled, then the white-black discrepancy becomes evident. In contrast with the finding by Bartel (1981), Mukerjee (2014) reported lower satisfaction than whites. Apparently, in the subsequent decades following Bartel (1981) study, a few other studies on the subject were recorded. For instance, using PhD level student, Bender and Heywood (2006) examined the job satisfaction and racial discrepancy in the United States. Their study documented a lower job satisfaction for black females in the business world while the black males in Academia were satisfied.

Similarly, Antecol and Cobb-Clark (2009) reported an increasing dissatisfaction in the US military. The study opined that racial bias was responsible for high turnover in the military. Since 1981, data availability have improved, hence the current study takes a step further to verify racial discrepancy among employees. Unlike earlier study which investigated job satisfaction base on gender and older males, current study investigates the relationship for both sexes and all ages above 18 years.

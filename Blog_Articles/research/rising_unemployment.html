Causes of Rising Unemployment Rate in the Uk and the Philippine

1.1 Introduction

One major challenge facing countries all over the globe is unemployment. About 160 million people are unemployed on the planet and 40% of which are young people (Oskooee & Mirzaie, 2007). It is defined as the quantity of individuals positively looking for job by the labour, which are prepared to work, and are looking for careers. The Uk Office for National Statistic (2017) reported the unemployment rate of people aged 16 and 64 between April and May 2017 to be 74.9%, a figure it regarded as the highest rate since comparable records began in 1971. The rate is forecasted to drift up slightly from current average of 4.5% in 2018 to 4.6% in 2019.

In Asia, the Philippines has the worst unemployment rate with 5.8% higher than countries like China 4%, Vietnam 2.3%, Indonesia 5.5%, Malaysia 3.5%, and Thailand 1% in 2016 (Ibon, 2018) while a government survey confirmed that more than 27.6 million Filipinos or 32.9% of the population are poor. Akindele 2006 views unemployment as a critical problem which many economies grapple with. In his study, he opined that the social consequences of unemployment are more perceived in developing and third world economies than the developed economies, reason being that support, subsidies and allowances are available to the unemployed in the developed climes.

However, while (Udabah Tunah 2010; Aurangzeb & Asif, 2013) noted technological variations, involvement of ladies in work force, demographic framework, economic circumstances, low production of electricity, settlement from rural area's towards cities, inadequate and inefficient use of human capital as the main reason for unemployment in many economies, Fadayomi (1992) and Osinubi (2006) documented that unemployment is a direct effect of inability to put to judicious and efficient use the manpower resources readily available especially in the rural sector.

Muhammad, Inuwa, and Oye 2011; Alanana 2003; MCgrath 2012 all found unemployment to be detrimental since it affects all facet of any economy. According to them, unemployment brings about some negative social and economic consequences to include increase in crime rate, loss of respect and identity, reduction in purchasing power, psychological injuries, corruption, loss of national output, negative multiplier effect, fiscal and social cost. He however reiterated that its effect varies across different climes since severity level differs too.

Previous studies Li and Liu (2012), Vermeulen (2015) and Yelwa, David, and Awe (2015) highlights common indices affecting unemployment rate to include inflation (INF), GDP growth (GDP), population (POP), exchange rate but has failed to reach a consensus. This study will further on the subject and so will attempt to highlight the causes and consequences of unemployment.

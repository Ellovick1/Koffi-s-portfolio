The Downstream: 2017, 1st Quarter refining capacity increases more than 2015 total – My Make on this

Published on May 18, 2017

https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/downstream-oil-gas-1st-quarter-refining-capacity-increases-koffi/

Defective policy framework, lack of political will, greed and corruption are vital factors that have been linked to the dismal performance of Nigeria’s refineries for decades. Maybe, the situation is about to change as the Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation (NNPC) through the Group Managing Director Maikanti Baru, reiterated that Nigerian refineries processed 10 million barrels of crude oil in the first quarter of 2017, more than they did for the whole of 2015 while briefing a team from the UK’s Royal College of Defense Studies, a news which has raised optimism by his audience

The country’s refineries meant to process about 445,000 barrels of oil per day have performed below par for decades thus resulting in frequent scarcity and escalated cost to her domestic consumers. In a dire need to meet the growing demand for domestic consumption which grows at about 13% annually, Nigeria imports refined crude at a rate higher than what she exports, a position which question Pareto efficiency is culminating to the cauldron of wastages and losses which runs into billions of dollars annually.

Rather fascinating it is, to note that despite its status as Africa’s top crude oil producer and exporter, Nigeria continues to trail the likes of Algeria, Egypt, Libya, and South Africa regarding refining capacity. Previous research reveals that Egypt has the highest primary refining among the five countries, followed by Algeria and South Africa, then Nigeria. A country like Singapore has no crude oil, yet has many refineries. Why can’t Nigeria be a refining hub for the rest of Africa? Exportation of refined crude rather than crude exportation should be the case because this is where the value is created.

For Nigeria, refining optimally was long overdue. Yes, domestic refining by itself is not a sufficient condition to guarantee product stability, but I consider it a necessary condition since there are definite gains from domestic crude processing to its outright importation. If and only if the statement made by the NNPC Boss, Baru is true then I say it is a course in the right direction. With the current political will and the fit registered, the downstream oil and gas seems promising; we can only hope for the best

Exercise and Diabetes

Aside from nutrition and medication, physical exercise has been considered as one of the fundamental options for treatment of diabetes mellitus since the past 100 years ago. Diabetes mellitus is a persistent metabolic illness, characterized by a surging blood-glucose level either as a result of a relative insulin deficiency or insulin resistance or both. Consequently, it may result in glycation of tissues, and acute metabolic inconvenience. In other cases, it damages critical body organs and causes acute severe health deteriorations. According to the global research study, there is an increasing prevalence of diabetes mellitus and continue to grow at an alarming rate annually.

Symptoms of diabetes include frequent urination, increased thirst, loss of weight, general body fatigue, consistently having a dry mouth and itchy skin, blurred vision, weak erection in men, miscarriages in women, etc. Health expert predicts that the most vulnerable people to diabetes are those with traits of this disease from family background, those with high blood pressure and stroke inclusive which could later lead to other health complications like kidney failure, heart problem, total blindness, and death.

Seemingly, there is a need for proper management to diagnose and treat diabetes mellitus and its associating complications. Diabetes Mellitus is classified into two basic categories to include the insulin-dependent commonly known as type 1 (T1DM), and non-insulin dependent diabetes mellitus otherwise called type 2 (T2DM). Evidence from studies and medical professional outlines diabetes management strategies to include the use of modern medicines, alternative or herbal medicines and exercise management therapy.

This array of management strategy impacts positively on the illness. However, the side effects of medications are also increasingly enormous thus; cannot be overlooked. This makes exercise an ideal treatment option for Diabetes Mellitus. To date, the place of exercise in the control of diabetes mellitus is extensive. Globally, researchers have considered the importance of exercise to diabetic patients, but admittedly, there is a lack of interest in performing exercise amongst diabetes mellitus patients. The need for proper understanding of this concept becomes imperative.

Exercise and the Diabetic Patient

Exercises are physical activity intended to improve the strength and fitness of an individual. They could be planned, structured, intentional and repetitive movement of body parts to achieve a good physique. Owing to the benefits accrued to the body, exercises are recommended to everyone including patients diagnosed with diabetes mellitus. Aerobic and resistance exercise are primary therapeutic effects towards diabetic mellitus.

Aerobic Exercise and T2DM

Aerobic exercise enhances oxygen utilization and improves cardiovascular and respiratory functions. Aerobic exercises are considered as excellent therapeutic options for the management of T2DM. It is essential for the physiological processes of the body and lowers the risk factors in insulin resistance diabetes Mellitus. Exercises in this category include cycling, treadmill, rope jumping, walking, swimming, rowing, running among others. Moderately, they give support to blood pressure in diabetic neuropathy patients.

However, intensive aerobic exercise improves the physiological parameters including glycemic control, fasting blood glucose level and lipid profile. It rehabilitates the endothelial function and slows down arterial stiffness; a precursor for cardiovascular complications in T2D. Apparently, exercise enhances increase glucose uptake through the glucose transporter 4 (GLUT4) to the skeletal muscle during the exercise account for reducing blood sugar level in diabetic patients. The death in insulin receptors weakens glucose uptake and GLUTA translocation but could be restored by exercise therapy

Resistance Exercise and Type 2DM

The resistance exercise effectively manages glucose and low insulin resistivity to T2DM. Just as the name, the resistance exercises are activities undertaken against a resistance. Exercises under the resistance exercise include weightlifting.

In contrast to the aerobic exercise, the resistance type depends on the apparatus. T2DM benefits from therapeutic regimes of the resistance exercise and this include As scheduled by the aerobic exercise, the resistance is safe for elderly insulin resistance diabetic patients. They improve the sensitivity of the insulin, daily dissipation of energy and life quality. Resistance exercises build the muscular strength, lean mass and the bone density which is often a precursor for functional status and glycemic management. They are also responsible for the prevention of the sarcopenia and osteoporosis.

Exercise and Type 1DM

Basically, all the levels of exercises consisting of leisure, sports, relaxation, and competitive shows and entertainments can be carried out by patients who have type 1 diabetes who are devoid of complications. More so, the patient’s must possess an excellent blood glucose control. The capacity to regulate and modify the therapeutic routines including medical nutrition and insulin to ensure safer participation has been noted vital management for these set of patients. Individuals. The patients in routinely self-monitors and collate blood glucose data to verify and examine his or her responses to the routine exercises.

Importance of Exercises to Diabetic Patients
  • Glycemic Management: Routine physical exercise improves carbohydrate metabolism and the sensitivity of insulin. They are ideal for patients with diminished glucose fluctuation or uncomplicated type 2 diabetes with acceptable adherence rates.
  • Prevention of cardiovascular disease: As a vital risk factor for coronary disease especially with concomitant hypertension, hyperinsulinemia, central obesity, and the overlap of metabolic abnormalities of hypertriglyceridemia, low HDL, modified LDL, and elevated FFA, the insulin resistance in the diabetic patient is sustained. It is most probable that a lot of the beneficial effects of exercise on cardiovascular risk are related to improvements in insulin sensitivity.
  • Hyperlipidemia: Routine exercises have been shown to be effective in lowering the levels of triglyceride-rich VLDL.
  • Hypertension: A relationship exist between insulin resistance and hypertension in patients. The impacts of exercise on reducing blood pressure levels have been proven to be most consistently in hyperinsulinemic issues
  • Fibrinolysis: A good number of Patient with T2DM have impaired fibrinolytic activity with high levels of plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1), Studies have demonstrated an association of aerobic fitness and fibrinolysis.
  • Obesity: Exercise enhances weight loss. It maintains weight when matched with effective calorie –controlled diet plan.
  • Prevention of T2DM: There is a great deal of evidence that exercises delays and prevents the onsets of type 2 diabetes for a patient who is type already.
How to Exercise with Diabetes as a Condition
  • Diabetic adults should undertake about 150 min or more of moderate-to-intense exercises weekly ideally spanning at least three days/week, with no more than two consecutive days without exercise. For younger and more for patients, fewer duration, minimum 75 min/week of vigorous intensity or training at interval may be adequate for type 2 and one diabetes
  • • Both aerobic and resistance exercise are recommended for most people with diabetes. Walking is often the most popular and most feasible type of aerobic exercise in overweight, middle-aged, and older adults with diabetes. For those who struggle with pain upon walking- due to osteoarthritis, semirecumbent cycling may provide an alternative. For most middle-aged individuals, moderately brisk walking on level ground or semirecumbent cycling would be an example of moderate aerobic exercise, while brisk walking up an incline or jogging would be vigorous aerobic exercise. Resistance exercise performed 2 or 3 times per week may provide benefits that complement those of aerobic training- increased strength and vigor, reduced body fat, increased resting metabolic rate.
  • 60 min/day or more of average or strenuous aerobic exercise, with muscle-strengthening, and bone-strengthening activities included at least three days/week for children and adolescents with type 1 or type 2
  • 2–3 sessions/week of resistance exercise on nonsuccessive days ideal for adults living with type 1 and two diabetes
  • Schedule an adjustable and balance training 2–3 times/week for aged adults living with diabetes. Owing to individual preferences, you may incorporate Yoga and tai chi to improve muscular strength.
  • Diabetic patients should be encouraged to improve their gross routine nonexercise physical activity to derive additional health benefits
  • For all exercise, participation should be supervised training. It is more beneficial than the nonsupervised type.
